In the Boise metropolitan area, where development pushes against the foothills and the water table fluctuates across the Treasure Valley, understanding subsurface conditions without breaking ground is a practical necessity. The IBC references ASTM D6431 for surface geophysical methods, and for good reason: electrical resistivity / VES (Vertical Electrical Sounding) delivers depth-resolved stratigraphy without the disturbance of drilling. Our technical team runs resistivity surveys across Ada County terrain—from the Boise Bench gravels to the finer sediments near the Boise River—to map clay lenses, identify perched water, and estimate bedrock depth. When combined with intrusive verification like SPT drilling, the resistivity profile becomes a calibrated cross-section that holds up under review. For projects near the Foothills fault system, we often pair VES with seismic refraction to correlate seismic velocity with resistivity values.

The transition from Boise Bench gravels to Lake Idaho silts is electrically distinct—resistivity contrasts of 10:1 are common, making VES a reliable tool for mapping this critical boundary.

Methodology and scope

The contrast between the arid benchlands south of the Boise River and the irrigated bottomlands near Garden City creates distinct challenges for electrical methods. Dry, coarse alluvium on the bench can push contact resistance high, requiring extended arrays and careful electrode coupling to get reliable data. Down in the valley, where clay-rich interbeds from ancient Lake Idaho deposits hold moisture, the resistivity drops sharply—but that same signal helps delineate stratigraphic boundaries with surprising clarity. A typical Boise survey uses a Schlumberger or Wenner array with electrode spacings from 2 to 200 meters, depending on target depth. For landfill investigations or contaminant plume mapping, we integrate the resistivity data with in-situ permeability testing to estimate hydraulic conductivity across the site. The method is non-invasive, leaves zero surface disturbance, and works on sloped terrain where a drill rig cannot safely access the target area.

Local considerations

Boise's growth since the 1990s has pushed residential and commercial development into areas where subsurface data is sparse—particularly along the foothills interface and in former agricultural parcels near Meridian and Nampa. These zones often contain undocumented fill, variable compressible silts, and shallow groundwater that complicates foundation design. A VES survey run before intrusive exploration flags these conditions early: a low-resistivity zone at 3 meters might be saturated clay that will require over-excavation, while a high-resistivity rise at 12 meters could indicate competent basalt or cemented gravels suitable for end-bearing piles. Skipping this reconnaissance step in heterogeneous Boise soils means the first sign of a problem arrives after excavation has started. For sites within the mapped liquefaction susceptibility zones of the valley, resistivity data feeds directly into the stratigraphic model used for liquefaction triggering analysis.

Associated technical services

01

Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES) for Stratigraphic Profiling

One-dimensional resistivity depth sounding using Schlumberger or Wenner arrays. Ideal for mapping the depth to bedrock, identifying the water table, and distinguishing between coarse Boise Bench gravels and fine-grained Lake Idaho sediments. Data is inverted with iterative smooth-model algorithms and presented as resistivity-versus-depth curves with interpreted lithology.

02

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT) for Lateral Mapping

Multi-electrode 2D resistivity imaging for cross-sections up to 300 meters in length. Used for contaminant plume delineation, landfill liner integrity assessment, and locating abandoned channels or paleochannels in the Boise River floodplain. Dipole-dipole and Wenner-Schlumberger arrays provide resolution from near-surface to 40 meters depth.

How deep can Vertical Electrical Sounding investigate in Boise soils?

Depth of investigation is controlled by the maximum electrode spacing. In Boise, where we commonly use Schlumberger arrays with AB/2 spacings up to 100 meters, we reliably reach depths of 30 to 50 meters. With extended arrays on open ground—like agricultural parcels in Canyon County—we can push investigation depth beyond 100 meters, though resolution decreases with depth.

Does the Boise Bench geology affect resistivity survey quality?

Yes, the dry gravels and cobbles on the Boise Bench have high contact resistance that can reduce current injection into the ground. Our field team addresses this by wetting electrode positions, using multiple electrodes per station, or switching to a Wenner array which is less sensitive to near-surface resistivity contrasts. The data quality remains high with proper field technique.

Can electrical resistivity locate the water table in the Treasure Valley?

Absolutely. The water table in the Boise area typically presents as a sharp resistivity decrease—from 100-300 Ohm-m in dry alluvium to 10-40 Ohm-m in saturated sediments. VES soundings conducted at different seasons can also track seasonal water table fluctuation, which is useful for basement waterproofing design and dewatering planning.

How long does a VES survey take on a typical Boise site?

A single VES sounding with a Schlumberger array takes approximately 45 to 90 minutes depending on maximum electrode spacing. A complete site survey with 4 to 6 soundings and preliminary field interpretation is usually completed in one working day. 2D ERT lines take longer—typically one to two days for a full setup, acquisition, and breakdown.
